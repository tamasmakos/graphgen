[EN] Mario | Draghi said from the NULL that Prime Minister of Italy. – President Metsola, Members of Parliament, dear citizens, I am truly happy to be here, in the heart of the cradle of European democracy. I would first like to pay tribute to the memory of David Sassoli, who presided over the European Parliament during extremely difficult years. Throughout the pandemic, Parliament continued to meet, debate, and decide, a testament to its institutional vitality and Sassoli's leadership. Sassoli never stopped working on what he called in his last speech to the European Council a "new project of hope" for "a Europe that innovates, protects, and enlightens." This vision of Europe is more necessary today than ever. I thank President Metsola and all of you for your contribution to advancing this vision every day. The war in Ukraine presents the European Union with one of the most serious crises in its history. A crisis that is simultaneously humanitarian, security, energy, and economic, and it is occurring while our countries are still grappling with the consequences of the greatest health emergency in the last hundred years. The European response to the pandemic has been united, courageous, and effective. Scientific research has delivered, with unprecedented speed, vaccines capable of slowing the spread of the disease and drastically reducing its severity. We have organized the most massive vaccination campaign in recent history, which has allowed us to save lives, get boys and girls back to school, and restart the economy. We approved Next Generation EU, the first major European reconstruction project, funded by everyone's contributions to meet everyone's needs. The same readiness and determination, the same spirit of solidarity, must now guide us through the challenges we face. The institutions our predecessors built over the past decades have served European citizens well, but they are inadequate for today's reality. The pandemic and the war have called upon European institutions to take on responsibilities they have never assumed before. The geopolitical landscape is rapidly and profoundly changing. We must move with the utmost speed. And we must ensure that managing the crises we are experiencing does not bring us back to square one, but enables a transition to a fairer and more sustainable economic and social model. We need a pragmatic federalism that embraces all areas affected by the ongoing transformations—from the economy to energy to security. I have spoken of a pragmatic federalism, but I must add that our European values of peace, solidarity, and humanity need defending more than ever. And this defense has never been more difficult for individual states, and will become increasingly difficult. We need not only a pragmatic federalism, but an ideal federalism. If this requires embarking on a journey that will lead to treaty revision, let us embrace it with courage and confidence. If we can draw the strength to take a step forward from the tragic events of recent years, if we can imagine a more efficient functioning of European institutions that allows for timely solutions to citizens' problems, then we will be able to deliver to them a Europe they can identify with with pride. Russia's aggression against Ukraine has called into question the European Union's greatest achievement: peace on our continent. A peace based on respect for territorial borders, the rule of law, and democratic sovereignty; a peace based on the use of diplomacy as a means of resolving crises between states; a peace based on respect for human rights, which were violated in Mariupol, Bukha, and everywhere else where the Russian military unleashed violence against defenseless civilians. We must support Ukraine, its government, and its people, as President Zelensky has called for and continues to call for. In a war of aggression, there can be no equivalence between those who invade and those who resist. We want Ukraine to remain a free, democratic, and sovereign country. Protecting Ukraine means protecting ourselves, it means protecting the project of security and democracy we have built together over the last seventy years. Helping Ukraine means, above all, working for peace. Our priority is to reach a ceasefire as soon as possible, to save lives and enable the humanitarian interventions for civilians that remain extremely difficult today. A truce would also give new impetus to negotiations, which have so far failed to achieve the desired results. Europe can and must play a central role in fostering dialogue. We must do so because of our geography, which places us alongside this war, and therefore on the front lines in addressing all its potential consequences. We must do so because of our history, which has shown us capable of building a stable and lasting peace, even after bloody conflicts. Italy, as a founding member of the European Union, as a country that deeply believes in peace, is ready to engage on the front lines to achieve a diplomatic solution. The war is already having a profound impact on our countries. Since the beginning of the conflict, approximately 5.3 million people have left Ukraine for the European Union – mostly women and children. This is more than double the number of refugees present in the Union at the end of 2020 – approximately 2.5 million. Italy believes in the European values of hospitality and solidarity. We have welcomed over 105,000 Ukrainian refugees, thanks to the generosity of families, volunteers, and non-governmental organizations—to whom I extend my deepest thanks. Other countries—including Poland, Romania, Germany, and Slovakia—have made even greater efforts. Many refugees want to return home soon, and some have already begun to do so. However, we do not know how the conflict will evolve, nor how long it will last. We must be ready to build on our initial momentum so that Ukrainian refugees can fully integrate into our societies. Economically, the conflict has caused instability in global supply chains and volatility in commodity and energy prices. Ukrainian food supplies have collapsed due to the devastation of the war and Russian export blockades of ports on the Black Sea and the Sea of Azov. Ukraine is the fourth largest foreign supplier of food to the European Union, sending us about half of our corn imports and a quarter of our vegetable oils. Russia and Ukraine account for over a quarter of global wheat exports. Nearly 50 countries worldwide depend on them for more than 30 percent of their imports. In March, prices of cereals and other major food commodities reached historic highs. There is a significant risk that rising prices, combined with reduced availability of fertilizers, will lead to food crises. According to the FAO, 13 million more people could suffer from hunger between 2022 and 2026 due to the war in Ukraine. Many countries, especially in Africa and the Middle East, are more vulnerable to these risks and could experience periods of political and social instability. We cannot allow this to happen. Our commitment, through development banks and multilateral financial institutions, and our bilateral engagement, must be maximal. Regarding energy, the price of crude oil, which fluctuated between $70 and $90 a barrel between December and January, is now hovering around $105, after peaking at $130 in March. The price of gas on the European market is around €100 per megawatt-hour—about five times higher than a year ago. These increases—which follow price increases already observed before the conflict began—have pushed inflation to levels not seen in decades. In the eurozone, the price index rose 7.5 percent in April compared to a year ago, significantly impacting household purchasing power and corporate production levels. The European economy is slowing: in the first three months of 2022, gross domestic product in the eurozone grew by 0.2 percent compared to the last four months of 2021. The International Monetary Fund forecasts that the European Union will grow by 2.9 percent this year, compared to the 4 percent estimated until recently. Each of these crises would require a strong response from the European Union. Their sum requires us to decisively accelerate the integration process. In the coming months, we must show European citizens that we are capable of leading a Europe worthy of its values, its history, and its role in the world. A stronger, more cohesive, and sovereign Europe, capable of taking its future into its own hands, as Chancellor Merkel said some time ago. Over the past 75 years, European integration has often been the best response—both practical and ideal—to common challenges. The founding fathers of the European Union understood that economic development and social progress were difficult to achieve through the resources of individual nation states alone. They identified the supranational model as the only one capable of uniting the interests of the European peoples and influencing events that would otherwise have been beyond their reach. Integration followed a gradual process, marked by crises and revivals, by successes achieved despite internal divisions and, at times, in the face of extreme resistance. It was a result built "piece by piece, sector by sector," to quote Robert Schuman, because the European Union could not be born "all at once, like an ideal city." To the traumas of the Second World War, Europe responded by creating the first institutions for economic cooperation. I think of the European Payments Union, which fostered the return to monetary stability and the recovery of trade, or the European Coal and Steel Community, which abolished customs barriers and other impediments to the free movement of goods in crucial sectors of the economy. The geopolitical tensions that arose with the Suez Crisis in 1956 helped accelerate the path towards the Treaties of Rome. Faced with the collapse of the Bretton Woods system in 1971, European countries responded by establishing the monetary snake and then the European Monetary System. They responded to the growing Euroscepticism of the 1980s with the targeted intervention programs proposed by the Delors Commission and the Single European Act of 1986. Following the end of the Soviet Union and the reunification of Germany, Europe followed up with the signing of the Maastricht Treaty, the creation of Monetary Union, and, finally, the European Union's eastern enlargement. The eurozone crisis in the early years of the last decade led to a strengthening and modernization of economic institutions, starting with the European Central Bank. The pandemic, as I mentioned earlier, brought us together and led to the creation of Next Generation EU. You see, this long journey of integration has changed our lives for the better, because it has given us peace, prosperity, and a social model to be proud of. The single market has not only revitalized the European economy in a time of difficulty, but has also ensured protections for consumers and workers, and forms of social security unique in the world. We have built common democratic institutions, like this Parliament, in which we can reach shared decisions and enforce respect for fundamental rights. We have made the European Union not only an economic space, but also a space for the defense of human rights and dignity. This is a legacy we must not squander, from which we cannot retreat. Now is the time to continue this journey. The Conference on the Future of Europe concludes on May 9, and the final declaration calls on us to be extremely ambitious. We want to be at the forefront of shaping this new Europe. In a geopolitical landscape that has suddenly become much more dangerous and uncertain, we must address the economic and social emergency and guarantee the safety of our citizens. Defense investments must be made with a view to improving our collective capabilities—as the European Union and NATO. The last European Council took an important decision with the approval of the "Strategic Compass," which we must implement rapidly. However, we must quickly move beyond these initial steps and build effective coordination between defense systems. Our security spending is approximately three times that of Russia, yet it is divided across 146 defense systems. The United States has only 34. This is a profoundly inefficient distribution of resources, which hinders the construction of a truly European defense. Strategic autonomy in defense requires, first and foremost, greater efficiency in European military spending. It is appropriate to convene a conference to rationalize and optimize our investments in military spending. Furthermore, the construction of a common defense must be accompanied by a unified foreign policy and effective decision-making mechanisms. We must move beyond the principle of unanimity, which gives rise to an intergovernmental logic of crossed vetoes, and move toward decisions taken by qualified majority. A Europe capable of making timely decisions is a more credible Europe in the eyes of its citizens and the world. An initial acceleration must concern the enlargement process. The full integration of countries expressing European aspirations does not pose a threat to the sustainability of the European project. It is part of its realization. Italy supports the immediate opening of accession negotiations with Albania and North Macedonia, in line with the decision taken by the European Council in March 2020. We want to give new impetus to negotiations with Serbia and Montenegro and ensure maximum attention to the legitimate expectations of Bosnia and Herzegovina and Kosovo. We support the accession of all these countries and we want Ukraine to join the European Union. We must follow the accession path we have outlined, but we must also proceed as quickly as possible. The solidarity shown towards Ukrainian refugees must then push us towards a truly European management of migrants arriving from other contexts of war and exploitation. More generally, it is necessary to define an effective European mechanism for managing migration flows, one that goes beyond the rationale of the Dublin Treaty. We must strengthen and make repatriation agreements truly effective, but we must also strengthen legal channels for entry into the European Union. In particular, we must pay greater attention to the Mediterranean, given its strategic position as a bridge to Africa and the Middle East. We cannot view the Mediterranean merely as a border area on which to erect barriers. Many young countries border the Mediterranean, ready to infuse their enthusiasm into their relationship with Europe. With them, the European Union must build a genuine partnership, not only economic, but also political and social. The Mediterranean must be a hub of peace, prosperity, and progress. Energy policy is an area in which Mediterranean countries must and can play a fundamental role for Europe's future. Europe faces a profound geopolitical reorientation destined to increasingly shift its strategic axis southward. The war in Ukraine has exposed the profound vulnerability of many of our countries to Moscow. Italy is one of the most exposed member states: approximately 40 percent of the natural gas we import comes from Russia. We have no coal, no nuclear power, and almost no oil. Such energy dependence is economically imprudent and geopolitically dangerous. Italy intends to take all necessary decisions to defend its own security and that of Europe. We supported the sanctions the European Union has decided to impose on Russia, including those in the energy sector. We will continue to do so with the same conviction in the future. In recent weeks, we have moved with the utmost speed and determination to diversify our gas supplies. And we have taken important simplification measures to accelerate the production of renewable energy, essential to making our growth more sustainable. The reduction in fossil fuel imports from Russia makes it inevitable that Europe will look to the Mediterranean to meet its needs. I am referring to gas fields, as a transitional fuel, but above all to the enormous opportunities offered by renewables in Africa and the Middle East. Southern European countries, and Italy in particular, are strategically positioned to harness this energy production and act as a bridge to northern countries. Our future vitality depends on the investments we make today. At the same time, we must immediately find solutions to protect families and businesses from rising energy costs. Reducing bills and fuel prices is also a way to make any sanctions more sustainable over time. Since the beginning of the crisis, Italy has called for a European price cap on gas imported from Russia. Russia sells nearly two-thirds of its natural gas exports to the European Union—largely through pipelines that cannot be redirected to other buyers. Our proposal would allow us to use our negotiating power to reduce the exorbitant costs currently weighing on our economies. At the same time, this measure would reduce the sums we send to President Putin every day, which inevitably finance his military campaign. We also want to structurally revise the electricity pricing mechanism, which depends on the production cost of the most expensive energy source, which is usually gas. Even in normal times, energy generation from fossil fuels has higher production costs than that from renewable sources. This is a problem that is only set to worsen over time. As the share of renewable energy in our energy mix gradually increases, prices will become less and less representative of the overall market's generation costs if we continue with this system. In this period of extreme volatility in the gas market, the price differential is disproportionate. Price increases in the gas market have spilled over into the electricity market, even though the production cost of renewables, from which we now obtain a significant portion of our energy, has remained very low. In Italy, in the first four months of this year, the price of electricity quadrupled compared to the same period last year, with a severe impact on the economy. The Italian government, as well as other governments, have reacted forcefully to protect businesses and families, especially the most vulnerable. Italy alone has spent approximately €30 billion this year alone. The emergency management of these price increases has many limitations, chief among them the sustainability of the public budget. The problem is systemic and must be addressed with structural solutions that break the link between gas and electricity prices. The issue of energy costs will be at the heart of the next European Council. Strong, immediate decisions are needed, for the benefit of all European citizens. The various crises arising from the conflict in Ukraine come at a time when Europe already faces enormous spending needs. The ecological and digital transitions require urgent investments. Added to these are the costs of war, which we must address immediately to prevent our continent from sinking into recession. In both cases, these are asymmetric costs, affecting different segments of the population and productive sectors differently and therefore requiring different compensatory measures. No national budget is capable of sustaining these efforts alone. No country can be left behind. Social peace on our continent is at stake, as is our ability to withstand sanctions, especially in those countries historically most dependent on Russia. The European Union has already developed several useful tools to manage these challenges. These are the responses we implemented during the pandemic, which ensured the European Union's rapid and widespread economic recovery. We must build on this success and adapt these same tools to the circumstances we face. SURE—the European instrument for temporary support to mitigate unemployment risks in an emergency—has provided loans to Member States to support the labor market. The European Union should expand its scope to provide countries that request it with new financing to mitigate the impact of rising energy prices. I am referring to measures to reduce energy bills, but also to temporary support for the lowest wages, such as we did yesterday, with tax relief measures for the lowest wage earners. These have the advantage of protecting the purchasing power of families, especially the most vulnerable. Using a loan mechanism like SURE would avoid the use of non-repayable grants to pay for national current expenditure measures. At the same time, in a period of rising interest rates, it would provide Member States with the most fragile public finances with a less expensive alternative to market borrowing. We could thus expand the scope of support measures and simultaneously limit the risk of financial instability. This measure should be implemented very quickly, as we have been in this situation for 8, 9, 10 months, to allow governments to intervene immediately to support the economy. Regarding long-term investments in areas such as defense, energy, food security, and industrial safety, the model is Next Generation EU. The system of scheduled payments, linked to timely checks on the achievement of objectives, offers a virtuous mechanism for monitoring the quality of spending. Spending the resources allocated to us wisely is essential for our credibility with European citizens and partners who, as I have said many times, have agreed to tax their citizens in order to help Italy and other countries that have used these grants. Good governance is not simply responding to current crises. It means acting immediately to anticipate future ones. The founders of Europe have shown us how to make democracy effective on our continent as it gradually transforms. European integration is the best ally we have to face the challenges that history presents us. Today, as at all crucial junctures since the post-war period, we need determination, vision, but above all unity. I am sure we will find them once again, together. (Applause)
