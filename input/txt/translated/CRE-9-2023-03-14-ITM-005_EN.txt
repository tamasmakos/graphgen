Although the clashes of the great powers of history have repeatedly brought painful tragedies, they have also highlighted in our memory the invaluable value of freedom and human dignity. It is symbolic that exactly 700 years ago, the ruler of Lithuania, Gediminas, addressed the European society of that time in writing, inviting foreign knights, merchants, artisans, farmers, priests and monks to come to Lithuania, where they would be able to freely profess the Christian faith and strive for a better life. From this date, January 25, 1323, we count the history of Vilnius, the capital of Lithuania. However, it is no less important that Gediminas' letters have established in our historical memory the ideal of Lithuania as an open, tolerant, progressive European country, which we want to strive for. Indeed, Lithuania has been an integral part of Europe for centuries and has maintained this fundamental connection even in the face of the most difficult trials, occupations and terror. The belief that sooner or later we will return to the community of independent European states built on the foundations of freedom, responsibility and trust sustained our hope during the years of captivity. This belief inspired me, like about 2 million other people in Lithuania, Latvia and Estonia, on August 23, 1989, to protest against the illegal Soviet occupation and the conspiracy of Stalin and Hitler. Having joined the 650 km long human chain – the Baltic Way – we together began our journey of liberation, and at the same time our return to Europe and opening up to the world. Today, it seems to me that the spirit of the Baltic Way shines extremely brightly in Ukraine's struggle for freedom, independence and a democratic future in Europe. As I stand here, I recall the warning voiced by Volodymyr Zelenskyy, the President of Ukraine and my dear friend, at the European Parliament a month ago: that Russia is seeking to destroy not only Ukraine, but the European way of life, to destroy by violence and lies all of us who cherish freedom, honor and dignity, who respect the rule of law and human rights. We cannot allow this to happen if we really care about the future of Europe. We must take a stand and show by our actions what is truly precious and valuable to us. Today, Ukraine is going through an even more painful experience as it defends itself against the bloody murders of Bucha, Irpin and Izyum. I am happy that Europe and other freedom-loving nations did not succumb to the lies, disinformation and intimidation spread by Russia. The total support of Lithuania, including non-governmental organizations, businesses and individuals, to Ukraine is approaching 1.5 percent of GDP.
