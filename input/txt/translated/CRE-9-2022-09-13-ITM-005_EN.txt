The crises of recent years, the pandemic, the war and now the energy crisis have shown that the need for solidarity and unity can in turn affect every Member State. Dear Members of the European Parliament, Mr. Metsola, Ukraine's place is in the European Union. In June, we demonstrated our straightforwardness and reliability as a partner when we made new decisions on the enlargement of the European Union. Ukraine and Moldova were granted EU candidate country status and Georgia was promised a European perspective. The Western Balkan countries are also finally moving forward with the Union. The path to membership is not short and the process is not quick. The doors of the European Union must still be open to all European countries that want to be part of our community of values ​​and are committed to making the necessary reforms. It is a question of the countries' own will to decide on their own future and place in the world. This is precisely the fundamental principle that Russia is attacking with its actions. As a result of Russia's war of aggression, Finland and Sweden have applied to become members of the defence alliance NATO, and our countries' NATO membership will strengthen the security of all of northern Europe and make the entire alliance stronger. (Applause) As a result of Russia's actions, the Western world is more united than ever. Russia is more alone than ever. Above all, we must continue to support Ukraine in all its forms and be prepared for even tougher sanctions. The stronger the impact of sanctions, the more expensive it will be for Russia to continue the war. Sanctions must also be visible in the everyday lives of ordinary Russians. It is not right that, while Russia is killing Ukrainian civilians, Russian tourists are traveling freely in Europe. The issuance of visas must be severely restricted. A complete suspension of the EU-Russia visa facilitation agreement is justified in this situation, but it is not a sufficient measure on its own. The availability and price of energy are the most important issues we need to resolve in the coming months and years. Together, we must do everything we can to ensure that ordinary citizens and businesses can survive the coming autumn and winter. In the short term, it is essential that we find all the means by which we can secure energy supplies and lower energy prices. It is important that the Commission presents proposals that can quickly address electricity prices, electricity market disruptions and related acute issues, such as problems on the electricity derivatives exchange. Conventional solutions are no longer enough. Solutions must be sought without prejudice and exceptional measures may be needed. In the medium and long term, our only way out of the energy crisis is to invest heavily in renewable and emission-free energy production, common European transmission networks and storage technologies. We must divest ourselves from Russian fossil energy as quickly as possible. Energy investments are also essential to respond to the climate crisis. Although the situation is far from what we hope for and will test every European country in an unprecedented way, the crisis also offers an opportunity for a better future. Now, at the latest, we must take a leap towards a more climate-sustainable economy. Green transition investments will improve our self-sufficiency and ensure the competitiveness of our society in the future. It is good that the Fit for 55 preparedness package has been taken forward with determination even during these difficult times. As the negotiations enter their final stages, it is important to ensure that the level of ambition of the preparedness package is maintained. At the same time, expectations towards the EU have increased even further. It is clear that the EU budget must be sufficient to ensure the Union's ability to act, even in crises. A capable and internally united Union is a credible actor in external relations as well. Responsibility for economic policy must continue to lie with the Member States themselves, in accordance with the Treaties. At the same time, it must be remembered that our economies are not separate from each other, but when one member state falls into recession, the effects are felt throughout the Union. Common rules of the game are needed and must be further developed. The most important task of the regulatory framework for fiscal policy is to contain the over-indebtedness of Member States and the associated risks. At the same time, the regulatory framework must take into account the impact of cyclical fluctuations on public finances and encourage counter-cyclical policies that smooth out cyclical fluctuations. The current regulatory framework does not provide sufficient incentives for this. Our common rules of the game are not just about fiscal policy, but key tools, such as the European Semester, must also be developed to take economic, social, employment and environmental issues into account better than before. A rules-based and responsible economic policy is needed, because only an economically strong and economically sustainable Europe has the ability to act independently and, if necessary, also help others, such as Ukraine now. Ladies and gentlemen, the EU has demonstrated its flexibility and ability to act in crises. Even in exceptional times, we worked resolutely for a better Europe. Promoting common goals and implementing previously agreed reforms despite crises is a testament to the EU’s ability to act under external pressure. At the same time, crises have also starkly exposed our vulnerabilities. The debate on Europe’s strategic autonomy is one of the most important debates we are currently having. It is about our ability to face external challenges and secure the functioning of our societies in all situations. When the pandemic swept over us, we realized how dependent we were on production chains outside Europe for medical supplies. We had to quickly build our capacity to secure the supply of protective equipment and other essential supplies. The war has shown how important it is for Europe to have its own defence production and how vulnerable we are in terms of energy. We must admit that we have been far too naive about Russia and have based our assumptions about Russia’s actions on erroneous perceptions. We should have listened more carefully to our friends from the Baltics and Poland, who lived under Soviet rule. Together, we are now paying a heavy price for our dependence on Russian energy. (Applause) War and the price of energy also threaten to drive the world into a food crisis. Europe must be sufficiently self-sufficient in food production and we must also help others. At its worst, a global food shortage will lead to famine, social unrest and migration. That is why Africa's cause is our cause. Although we are now focusing on energy and other acute issues, we must also be able to prevent future crises. One of the most important issues in the future is our technological capability and know-how. Our societies will be fully digitalised, and we cannot afford to make the same mistakes we have made with energy. We cannot rely on authoritarian countries to develop critical technologies or in production chains. Europe must strengthen our technological capability together with its democratic partners. There is no room for complacency. This is not just about the economy, but also about our security and the functioning of our societies. Dear Europeans, although there are significant challenges ahead, I would like to emphasise that in the long arc of history, European cooperation is a success story. We must change – to be better, bolder and even more capable. The European Union's ability to act is fundamentally about political will. Our citizens did not ask for institutional changes, but for reforms that respond to the greatest challenges facing humanity and their everyday concerns. We can meet these needs already within the current framework, for example by increasing qualified majority voting in the area of ​​common foreign and security policy. Honourable Members of the European Parliament, dear Europeans, we are not living through our first or last crisis. Despite the difficult times, and precisely for this reason, we must rely on our greatest strength: our mutual trust and unity. In order to be strong externally, we must take care of our common values ​​internally – the rule of law, democracy and human rights. By acting in accordance with these principles, we will also overcome future crises. (Applause)
