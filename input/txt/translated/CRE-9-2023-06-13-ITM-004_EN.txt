[EN] Nikos | Christodoulides said from the NULL that President of the Republic of Cyprus. – Madam President, honourable Members of the European Parliament, Mr Vice-President of the Commission, I would like to thank the President for her kind invitation to be here today, more than three months after the assumption of the Presidency of the Republic of Cyprus, to speak before the European Parliament, before all of you, about how I envision the present and future of our Union, the present and future of our European citizens. How I believe that together we can face common challenges and develop our Union even further and more unitedly for a brighter future. This emblematic Plenary Chamber of the Parliament, here in Strasbourg, is quite familiar to me. And perhaps it is this previous course of closeness and synergy with Parliament that creates in me today not only a feeling of emotion, but above all, a sense of responsibility, now that my presence here is in my capacity as Head of State of a Member State and as a member of the European Council. It is an extraordinary honour for me to address you, the elected representatives of the European citizens, here, where the heart of European democracy beats with unceasing and strong pulses. It is an honour that entails a debt, because I know first-hand the decisive role you play in terms of the quality of life from one end of our 27 countries to the other, but also in terms of the prospect of prosperity, peace and security for the entire planet. Ladies and gentlemen, I am one of those who firmly believe that the creation of the European Union is the most important collective achievement on our continent since the Age of Enlightenment. The political vision of eminent men and women to unite our continent through the creation of the institutions of the European Union, in order to establish peace and to guarantee the prosperity and security of our peoples, remains more relevant today than ever. At the same time, I believe that the work of these great men and women who charted a course has not yet been completed. However, it is on a continuous, evolutionary path and it is now in our own hands how it will proceed. We must treat the edifice that has been handed over to us as a legacy and an achievement that we must strengthen, and this requires vision, courage and determination. It is not our choice, but our duty, to work for a Union that becomes stronger every day through institutional deepening, with more common policies, with the Member States transferring an ever greater part of our national sovereignty to common supranational institutions with the aim of well-being, peace, security and prospects for every European citizen, regardless of the country or region they live in. Ladies and gentlemen, I truly believe that the European Union is the greatest model of peace and prosperity, after a horrific first half of the twentieth century that unfortunately cost the lives of millions of people on our continent. I am fully aware as a current European leader that we have a duty to develop this model of peace bequeathed to us by the great visionaries Schuman, Monnet, Altiero Spinelli, Paul-Henri Spaak and Simone Weil. I am a supporter and enthusiast of a targeted, step-by-step path aimed at building a European Union with purely federal characteristics. This is in our collective interest in today's difficult and constantly changing international landscape, where the challenges are common and therefore require a common and effective response. As Member States of the European Union, it is not enough to look forward to how we will survive in today's international context with intense geopolitical competition. As Member States of the European Union, we must create the conditions to be protagonists, united in our diversity and utilizing our strong human potential, sharing our common principles and values, on which our European edifice is firmly founded. I am absolutely convinced that we can make the European Union a protagonist and a pioneer in international developments. Ladies and gentlemen, after the tragedy of the great destructive wars of the first half of the 20th century, with the aim of never experiencing such conditions again, some dared and envisioned and laid the foundation for a united Europe. They understood very correctly that strength lies in our unity. They understood correctly that power lies in union. We are building on this legacy by strengthening unity, so that it is unshakable, because this unity will be the basic tool, our strength, our compass for the future we want, for Europe and for our citizens. We proposed and utilized this unity, and I am proud of it, in order to face the recent successive crises that have tested us and continue to test us to this day. Ladies and gentlemen, Cyprus, at the southeastern tip of what is now the European Union, has been an integral part of what historically began to be called Europe since ancient times. The beginning of our historical, economic, cultural and other ties with other peoples of Europe can be traced back millennia and since then these ties have been constantly developing, having become inseparable. The accession of Cyprus to the European Union, in the spring of 2004, a historic day that continues to live in my memory and in the memory of all my compatriots, was what had long been expected. It was a highly natural development. It was a form, if you will, of returning to the space to which we belong, through the formalization of the already existing strong historical ties of Cyprus with continental Europe. I continue to believe that our accession to the European Union in 2004 is the most important historical event since the establishment of the Republic of Cyprus in 1960 and marks our return home, to our common European home. Today, after almost 20 years of progress, Cyprus is an active member of the European Union. Within this context, the effective exercise of the rotating Presidency of the Council of the European Union by Cyprus, in the first semester of 2026, is a very important milestone in the term of my government. I was blessed to actively participate in the first Presidency of Cyprus in 2012 and I have no doubt that the next Presidency by Cyprus will also be a very important milestone for both the European Union and my country. I assure you today that Cyprus will make the most of this opportunity to contribute in a completely positive manner and as an honest broker to ensuring institutional cohesion and to promoting as many legislative files as possible that will expand the added value of the European unification process and will be beneficial for European citizens. Furthermore, we are already examining specific, essential and necessary political initiatives that I would like to undertake within the framework of our Presidency in relation to our political cooperation as a European Union with the Mediterranean and the wider Middle East. Honourable Members of the European Parliament, my recent election to the Presidency of the Republic of Cyprus, without the support of the two major parties, but with the decisive support of civil society and smaller political forces and movements, was based on a people-centred programme. A modern program, based on the working model of the European Union and good practices from other member states. This is a model based on what our active participation in the European Union has taught us for so many years. My goal is the well-being of the citizens of Cyprus, peace and security. My election was the confirmation of society's need for change, so that society's problems can be truly heard and solutions can be launched, so that Cyprus and the Cypriot European citizen can see their everyday life with optimism and envision their future with conditions of equality for all citizens in work, education, health, for a smooth transition to a greener, more digital, modern and equal society. I listened to society and immediately upon my election I began the implementation of such a program, which for me constitutes my social contract with the Cypriot people. With the next European elections for the members of the European Parliament now visible on the horizon, I believe that we have a duty to listen carefully to citizens again, to seek to bring them closer to European affairs or rather to convince them that we are their voice. We do not maintain distances, we are one with them and the goal is common. It is the prosperity and security of all European citizens, respect for the rights of all and equal opportunities. We must regain the trust of our citizens in the great European ideal of peace, cooperation, solidarity and prosperity. To achieve this, we must first do more to ensure that European citizens know what the European Union is doing for their security and daily lives and we may need to communicate better what is being done and will be done. Our citizens should feel the changes that are being made with effort, whether they concern the economy, employment, the green transition, the digital transformation, or migration issues. We must be credible and politically responsible and we must give value and follow-up to the suggestions that citizens themselves have submitted right here, in the Plenary Hall of the European Parliament. This must be our guide and vision for the next steps that we will all take together. Within the same framework, I strongly believe that the European Union must be strategically autonomous in areas such as security, energy, health and elsewhere. This does not mean that the European Union will isolate itself or that we will turn our backs on our traditional, natural allies - quite the opposite. But today, more than ever, we need a European Union that is able to effectively address the challenges and thus be a more valuable and equal partner and ally for its friends. Strategic autonomy is something that our collective interest demands in today's difficult and ever-changing international context, as the condemnable Russian invasion of Ukraine reminds us. Regrettably, 17 months since the illegal Russian invasion of Ukraine, the war continues. It is therefore crucial that we equally continue our support to the heroic people of Ukraine. Let me be clear, dear friends: we will never allow border changes stemming from violence and war. We will never accept the result of Russia’s aggression against an independent sovereign state. It is a matter of principle and a priority to ensure that anarchy and the law of the jungle will not prevail over international law and the international rules-based order. The European Union ought to be at the forefront of upholding international law and the UN Charter. This is, after all, an inherent part of its foundation and of the European Union Treaties. For Cypriots in particular, who themselves have been victims of foreign invasion and an almost half—century—long occupation by Turkey, there is no other choice. From day one of Russia's aggression against Ukraine, Cyprus has maintained an unequivocal, principled stance in support of the independence, sovereignty, and territorial integrity of Ukraine. This is the path on which we will continue. Cyprus will continue to support Ukraine, including through the European Peace Facility and the EU monitoring assistance mission. We have been sending to Ukraine the biggest humanitarian aid package ever assembled on the island. We are also hosting a large number of Ukrainian refugees relative to our population, and we will continue to do so. Cyprus will be their safe haven for as long as necessary. Within the European Union, we exhibited unprecedented unity in response to this war. Our unity proved to be our most important tool. We have taken painful decisions that have come at a cost for our peoples and societies. But it is a cost worth shouldering. In unity we need to continue to jointly address issues that affect Ukraine but also have repercussions beyond EU borders. It has become blatantly and painfully clear that peace on the European continent cannot be taken for granted, that we must defend it and that a threat to peace somewhere in Europe is a threat to each and every country in Europe. Russia's military aggression against Ukraine has also upended energy markets, triggering price volatility and energy insecurity across the world. A crucial new element in the European political response to this situation is the REPowerEU Plan, building on the full implementation of the European Green Deal. As a result, both the European Union and its Member States are dynamically reshaping their energy strategies to reflect new geopolitical realities and to address the need for affordable energy. This includes intensified actions to increase gas supplies from the European Union's trusted partners. Cyprus, the only Member State in the Eastern Mediterranean region with confirmed gas reserves, and with historically excellent relations and long-standing partnerships in the field of energy with the vast majority of its neighbors, can play a key role in our joint efforts to address the energy crisis, by providing a reliable alternative energy corridor to Europe, comprising diverse sources and routes. We are of the view that the presence of major oil and gas companies in the region could be leveraged to create synergies towards a sustainable regional gas market that could supply the European Union with gas in the immediate future and with hydrogen in a few years' time. For this reason, we are intensifying our discussions with all major oil and gas companies to implement our vision to connect these different gas fields in the region, via pipelines, to a modular liquefaction plant located in Cyprus, through which gas can be exported to Europe. In addition, the East Med region has recently become much more proactive in pursuing and enhancing its energy goals towards a green transition. Both Israel and Egypt are progressing with important renewable energy projects to export renewable energy as well as to use it internally. As such, in 5-10 years' time, the regional electricity interconnections currently in the pipeline could contribute significantly to the supply of renewable energy from the East Med region to the European market. Maintaining a stable and secure environment in the Eastern Mediterranean is to the benefit of the region and its people, but also of strategic importance for the European Union. It is therefore of critical importance for the Union to continue sending clear and firm messages of deterrence to potential spoilers in the region, urging them not to proceed to new provocations and to engage constructively in the efforts aiming at reaching solutions in a peaceful and sustainable way, always based on international law. Dear friends, further to the energy equation of the Eastern Mediterranean, one must acknowledge that the common and pressing challenges the region faces can be addressed effectively only by acting collectively. Cyprus, as a pillar of stability and security in a volatile region, actively promotes its regional cooperation policy through the trilateral mechanisms of cooperation it established with Greece and other countries of the region in an effort to create regional synergies and partnerships. These regional collaborations have yielded tangible results through the consolidation of regional partnerships that contribute to strengthening our relations at the political level, as well as expanding and deepening them in several sectors, such as energy, security and defense, environmental protection, natural disasters and cultural heritage. It goes without saying that no country is excluded from such networks of regional cooperation, provided that they respect international law. Further strengthening of our relations with the countries of the Mediterranean, the Middle East, North Africa and the Gulf will remain a key pillar of Cyprus' foreign policy. This empowers Cyprus to have an active role as a geopolitical gateway between the European Union and the MENA region. We believe that Cyprus' regional policy adds value to the European Union's common foreign and security policy. Our political ties with the countries of the Southern Neighborhood allow us to bring strategic knowledge in the relevant European Union discussions, while at the same time we are able to convey key messages of their specific concerns to our European Union partners, and vice versa. Cyprus' geo-political position and its proximity to the continents of Asia and Africa is another determining factor, most recently evident when the island was used as a safe transit hub in the evacuation efforts from Sudan. This was not the first time Cyprus has assisted in such operations, with the most extended one being during the Lebanon crisis of 2006, when tens of thousands were evacuated and provided with humanitarian assistance, proving the lasting contribution of Cyprus as a reliable stakeholder and as a safe harbor in the region. We shall continue to contribute our utmost whenever needed. Despite the challenges the region is faced with, we are also witnessing that the Middle East and the Eastern Mediterranean are on a positive, transformative trajectory. The changing political dynamics in our neighbourhood, such as the recent rapprochement between Iran and Saudi Arabia, the Abraham Accords, the establishment of the East Mediterranean Gas Forum in Cairo and the maritime border deal between Israel and Lebanon constitute indicative examples of this rapidly evolving geopolitical mosaic. The wider Middle East and Gulf is not just Cyprus's neighborhood. It is the European Union's neighbourhood, and a vital one at that. We need to acknowledge with courage and honesty that as a European Union we have not invested as much as we ought to in this very important region. We ought to boldly work on making it a strategic priority to engage on a positive agenda with our partners in the region, whom we must see as equal partners, to assert a more active, extrovert role, so as to effectively reinforce the European Union's ties with the southern neighbors, and in doing so promoting peace and stability in the region. Dear friends, turning to migration, a crisis that is also linked to this important region, we must take into consideration the overall migratory pressures that Europe is facing, with certain Member States, including Cyprus, being at the forefront. In fact, Cyprus is the Member State with the highest percentage of first-time asylum applications in relation to its population. The military occupation of 37% of the territory of Cyprus constitutes an aggravating factor that hinders efforts towards sound management of flows, since control of those entering the country is practically impossible. Consequently, Cyprus is particularly vulnerable to the instrumentalization practices used by third countries, and this is clear from statistical data showing that over 90% of the migrants applying for asylum have departed from Turkey. The government is committed to take all necessary action to improve infrastructure for the reception of asylum seekers and expedite examination procedures, but also to increase the rate of returns of those who do not have any legal right to remain in Cyprus, making use of the support provided by the European Union and its agencies, which we greatly appreciate. Furthermore, the government is in close contact with the Commission, which is preparing a dedicated action plan for the Eastern Mediterranean migratory route. Domestically, we are also introducing a bill next week for the establishment of a Deputy Ministry for Immigration, Asylum and Integration, so as to better coordinate and implement our policies, which also of course include policies for effectively integrating legally residing third-country nationals. However, it will be difficult to achieve tangible and sustainable results in the absence of addressing the root causes of the phenomenon, or in the absence of a coordinated response at the European Union level, one that will convince Turkey to honor the obligations already assumed towards all Member States. Last week, the Council reached a general approach on two crucial aspects of the new Pact for Migration and Asylum. The difficulty of the negotiations demonstrates how complex the issue is for all Member States, and how many concessions had to be made in order to reach this agreement. Having said that, I hope the discussions with the European Parliament will proceed quickly. For the pact to work, it is imperative to maintain a balance between solidarity and the principle of responsibility, and to keep working hard on the external aspects of migration so as to reduce irregular flows. Dear friends, an integral part of the promotion of peace in the region of the Eastern Mediterranean is our effort to reunify Cyprus through a comprehensive settlement in line with international law, UN Security Council resolutions and the European Union’s law, values ​​and principles. Peace in Cyprus with the European Union at the forefront will send a resounding message of peace in a region that desperately needs it. The European Parliament has always supported Cyprus and its struggle to resolve the Cyprus problem, a problem of invasion and occupation that is still an open wound in the body of the whole of Europe. I thank you on behalf of the Cypriot Government, on behalf of the Cypriot people, for all the efforts, for the very important resolutions for us, including of course the resolutions on Famagusta, for the approval and increase of your financial assistance in relation to the investigations into the issue of missing persons. I also thank you for the sincere and personal interest of the President of the European Parliament, as well as for the relevant action of the political groups in support of our efforts to end the occupation and reunify our homeland. The reunification of Cyprus, ladies and gentlemen, through a comprehensive and sustainable solution, always within the framework of the agreed United Nations framework, is the highest priority of my government. 49 years of occupation and division in the heart of Europe is too many. I assure you that I will make every effort to achieve the resumption of the talks, always within the framework of the agreed solution and from the point where they left off in the summer of 2017 in Crans Montana. To this end, I have held a number of meetings, I have discussed with the Secretary-General of the United Nations and other officials, with the Presidents of the European Union institutions, with my counterparts as heads of state and of course with the Turkish Cypriot leader. In all my meetings, I convey the necessity for a new approach. My proposal essentially provides for enhanced involvement and a strengthened role of the European Union in the efforts both to break the deadlock and in the context of the resumption of talks. I want to take this opportunity to clarify, to avoid any misunderstanding, that we do not want any change in the context of the solution, which is always the relevant Security Council resolutions, which for us are the safety net in the Turkish approach on two states. Nor do we want the European Union to replace the United Nations. On the contrary, we wish the European Union to function complementary and subsidiary to the United Nations, to be utilized as a reunifying tool, as is the nature of the Union, unifying, fundamental to the conditions of peace and respect for the human rights of all. This is, moreover, what both the treaties of the European Union and the European acquis itself advocate. The Republic of Cyprus is a member state of the European Union and the Cyprus problem is a European problem that requires European solutions. The Union has every interest, but also an obligation, to contribute actively through a leading role for the definitive settlement of the Cyprus problem, utilizing all the political and economic means and tools at its disposal, in the broader context of Euro-Turkish relations. I am ready to discuss a gradual and mutually beneficial package, within the framework of Euro-Turkish relations, which will develop in parallel with the negotiations for a solution to the Cyprus problem on the agreed basis. My proposal also includes the appointment of a high-ranking European political figure as the European Union envoy for the Cyprus problem. I believe that such a figure would contribute greatly, especially at the present juncture, to breaking the deadlock and resuming the talks. It goes without saying that if we achieve the goal of returning to the talks, the European Union should also assist at a technocratic level, as it has done during the previous round of negotiations. In fact, the presence of the European Union had contributed greatly to the achievement of numerous convergences for the benefit of all the Cypriot people, including Greek Cypriots and Turkish Cypriots, and its involvement was decisive in bringing us very close to a solution to the Cyprus problem in 2017. At this moment, however, the presence of the European Union is considered essential, especially as efforts are focused on breaking the deadlock. The Cyprus problem can constitute a new successful model of peace for the European Union, so that the Union can demonstrate in practice that it has the unifying forces and the toolbox and can become a foundation for peace and security. This is what we are looking forward to and will fight for with all our might. In this, we want all of you here in the European Parliament as allies, strong supporters of this approach. Ladies and gentlemen, I would like to conclude my intervention today by underlining the utmost importance of the participation of the Republic of Cyprus as an equal member state of the European Union. In the past, I have emphasized it many times, and I have said it again today. Our accession to the European Union constitutes the greatest historical achievement since the establishment of the Republic of Cyprus. 19 years since our accession, I believe that despite the difficulties and problems that our European family faces from time to time, Cyprus's participation in the Union constitutes a strong political and diplomatic shield to counter any attempts against our national sovereignty and independence. The ongoing division of Cyprus, as a result of the Turkish occupation, unfortunately constitutes the greatest political anachronism within the otherwise most modern union of sovereign states on our planet. Jean Monnet had as one of his guiding principles the solution of problems by adding another dimension to an issue, thus diversifying the initial framework of the problem. “You change the context, you change the problem”, he emphasized. I firmly believe that through the more active institutional involvement of the European Union and the diversification of the framework of the problem, also involving the Euro-Turkish dimension, we can indeed break the current impasse and resume the negotiations from the point where they were interrupted in 2017, with the sole aim of a comprehensive solution to the Cyprus problem. We are not demanding, ladies and gentlemen, anything less and nothing more than what the citizens of the other Member States of the European Union enjoy. The Cypriot refugee, the Cypriot citizen, Greek Cypriot, Turkish Cypriot, Maronite, Armenian and Latin, is entitled to enjoy in his homeland the same rights, the same basic freedoms that any European citizen enjoys in Europe. This is what we aspire to, of course. This is what we are absolutely convinced that the European Union can indeed become a catalyst for peace. You change the context, you change the problem. I conclude by saying that for me and my government, a strong European Union also means a strong Cyprus. A strong Cyprus also means a stronger Europe. My own political commitment is that I will work tirelessly, both within the framework of my participation in the European Council and more broadly, so that the European Union remains coherent and united during this difficult period that we are going through, with geopolitical instability and the war that has unfortunately returned to our continent. I decided to run for President of the Republic of Cyprus as an independent candidate, without the support of any party. I was elected against the odds – the first time a President of Cyprus was elected without the support of one of the two big political parties. I know how to make brave, bold moves against the odds, and I believe, with engagement from all parties and from the European Union, I can be the President to solve the Cyprus problem. My conviction is unshakable that by remaining faithful to the founding principles and values ​​of our Union, we can look forward to better days. We can look forward to an even better, stronger European Union, for the benefit of our citizens and above all for the benefit of the new generation of our country, who watch us every day and cry out to us for hope. Thank you very much. (applause)

I want to send the message that we have a historical duty to work together so that this edifice of peace and prosperity, our European family, what was handed down to us, we can also hand it down to our children, to the new generations, bigger, more mature and stronger. And I am strongly convinced that we can achieve this. What I can tell you with certainty today, as an elected representative of the Cypriot people, is that I will make every effort as a member of the European Council to achieve the further integration of the European Union. I am a devotee, I strongly believe in the need for further integration and I will do everything possible on our part to achieve this institutional deepening with more common policies. All of which will lead the European Union to be a leading player at the international level. My vision for a more integrated Europe also passes through a reunited Cyprus. We know very well that we cannot change geography. We cannot change or choose our neighbors. I am very aware of the difficulties in relation to the Cyprus problem, but at the same time my vision for a stronger Europe passes through a reunited, a strong Cyprus that will be an even more active member of the European Union. It is for this reason that I want to close my speech by taking advantage of my presence in the European Parliament. I want to send a message to our Turkish Cypriot compatriots who are citizens of the Republic of Cyprus, they are citizens of the European Union. Their future, as well as that of the Greek Cypriots and the Armenians and the Latins and the Maronites, of all, of the entire Cypriot people, passes through our common status as a member state of the European Union. And for me, as President of all Cypriots, my greatest achievement, my greatest legacy, will be to hand over to our children a reunited Cyprus, a reunited state that will be an even stronger partner in a stronger European Union. Thank you very much. (applause)
